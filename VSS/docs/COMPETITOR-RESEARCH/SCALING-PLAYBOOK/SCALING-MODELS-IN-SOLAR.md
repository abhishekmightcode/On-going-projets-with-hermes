# Scaling Models in Solar Business — How Solar Companies Grow

---

## Model 1: Vertical Integration (VSS Style)

**Description:** Company handles everything in-house — lead generation, design, supply, installation, after-sales.

**Examples:** VSS, EcoSoch, most mid-sized Indian solar companies

**Pros:**
- Higher margins per project
- Full quality control
- Customer relationship ownership
- Brand building easier

**Cons:**
- Capital intensive (inventory, tools, vehicles)
- Hiring and training overhead
- Slower to scale geographically
- Team management complexity

**Growth lever:** Hire more project leads, invest in tools, open new offices

---

## Model 2: Franchise / Partner Network (ZunSolar Style)

**Description:** A central brand provides: brand, marketing, tech platform, training. Partners provide: local relationships, execution.

**Examples:** ZunSolar (75+ cities), Loom Solar (dealer network)

**Pros:**
- Fast geographic expansion
- Lower capital cost
- Brand + technology leverage for partners
- Recurring revenue from partner fees / commissions

**Cons:**
- Quality control is HARD — partner failures damage brand
- Requires strong ops playbooks
- Margin sharing with partners
- Brand dilution risk

**Growth lever:** Recruit partners in new cities, provide leads via platform

---

## Model 3: Lead Aggregator / Platform Model

**Description:** Company generates leads (via IndiaMART, website, app) and sells them to installers, or matches customers to installers.

**Examples:** IndiaMART, Justdial, Zunpulse (partial)

**Pros:**
- Low capital cost
- Fast scale
- Asset-light
- Can build CRM/platform revenue

**Cons:**
- Thin margins on lead sales
- Low customer loyalty
- Dependent on Google Ads / SEO for traffic
- Installers get trained and leave platform

---

## Model 4: IoT + SaaS Monitoring Platform

**Description:** Company sells solar systems but wraps them with IoT monitoring hardware + app. Recurring revenue from monitoring / alerts.

**Examples:** ZunSolar (built-in), Senseg (IoT-only)

**Pros:**
- Differentiated offering
- Post-installation sticky (app engagement)
- AMC renewal revenue
- Data for predictive maintenance

**Cons:**
- Hardware + software R&D investment
- Connectivity issues (rural/urban)
- Customer app fatigue — will they use it?

---

## Model 5: Government / B2G Focus

**Description:** Company focuses on government tenders, solar park allocations, EESL contracts.

**Examples:** Many large Indian EPC companies

**Pros:**
- Large project sizes
- Government payment (usually)
- Prestige + reference value

**Cons:**
- Long payment cycles (1–3 years)
- Tender-heavy bureaucracy
- Requires large teams for bid management
- Not suitable for VSS at current size

---

## Model 6: Solar Farming / Landowner Model

**Description:** Company develops solar farms on leased land. Revenue from selling power to DISCOM or third parties.

**Examples:** Orange Energy (mentions solar farming), large solar developers (ReNew, Azure, Adani)

**Pros:**
- Long-term lease income for company
- Large capacity installations
- Works with DISCOM revenue model

**Cons:**
- Land acquisition complexity
- High capital for panels
- Grid connectivity challenges
- Not residential / SMB focused

---

## Model 7: AMC / Maintenance Retainer Model

**Description:** Company focuses on post-installation maintenance, cleaning, inverter service, monitoring.

**Examples:** Companies that survived the 2018–2020 solar bust by pivoting to service

**Pros:**
- Recurring revenue
- High margin (service visits)
- Customer stickiness
- Low inventory risk

**Cons:**
- Requires service team (not sales team)
- Low ticket size per visit
- Scheduling and logistics complexity

---

## Model 8: Corporate / ESG B2B Model

**Description:** Company targets corporate clients (Google, Amazon, Samsung-type companies) for large rooftop or solar parking structures. Provides ESG reporting.

**Examples:** Tata Power Solar, large EPC firms

**Pros:**
- Large project sizes (₹50 lakh – ₹5 crore per project)
- ESG / sustainability narrative
- Recurring corporate clients

**Cons:**
- Long sales cycles (6–18 months)
- Corporate procurement bureaucracy
- Requires scale and balance sheet
- VSS currently too small for this

## Model Comparison for VSS

| Model | Fit for VSS | Recommendation |
|-------|------------|----------------|
| Vertical Integration | ✅ Current | Keep — core strength |
| Franchise / Partner | ⚠️ Medium-term | Consider after 50+ installs/month |
| Lead Aggregator | ❌ Low | Avoid unless partner-only model |
| IoT + SaaS | ⚠️ Medium | Add monitoring to current offering |
| Government / B2G | ❌ Too early | Avoid for now |
| Solar Farming | ⚠️ Niche | Could try 1–2 pilot projects |
| AMC / Maintenance | ✅ High | Build AMC revenue aggressively |
| Corporate / ESG | ❌ Too early | Target after ₹1 crore/month revenue |

## VSS Scaling Roadmap (Recommendations)

### Phase 1: Now (Current)
- **Keep vertical integration** — this is VSS's moat
- **Build AMC revenue** — every installation should have an AMC sold
- **Improve Google rating** — push for 5-star reviews actively
- **Document execution** — Executionflow is already built; refine it

### Phase 2: 6–12 Months
- **Add monitoring** — offer ZunSolar-style app-based monitoring as upsell
- **RWA campaigns** — target apartment societies with group rates
- **Hire 1–2 more project leads** — scale execution capacity

### Phase 3: 12–24 Months
- **Partner network** — expand beyond Bangalore via dealer partnerships
- **Solar farming** — pilot 1–2 landowner projects
- **Corporate B2B** — target mid-size commercial clients (shops, restaurants, factories)
